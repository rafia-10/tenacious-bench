# Synthesis Memo: Best Practices and Lessons Learned on Synthetic Data for Language Models (Liu et al., COLM 2024)

**Paper:** Liu et al., "Best Practices and Lessons Learned on Synthetic Data for Language Models," COLM 2024.
**Memo author:** Rafia
**Date completed:** 2026-04-29
**Assigned reading:** Common to all paths

---

## Core Argument

The paper surveys synthetic data generation techniques across four stages of the LM pipeline (pre-training, instruction tuning, preference learning, evaluation) and identifies six recurring lessons: (1) quality beats quantity at instruction scale, (2) diversity of generation seeds matters more than volume, (3) self-instruction pipelines degrade without a quality filter, (4) in-domain contamination is hard to prevent in large-scale synthesis, (5) evaluation data requires harder contamination controls than training data, and (6) multi-model routing improves diversity over single-model generation.

---

## Three Principles I Apply to Tenacious-Bench

### 1. Quality over quantity (Section 3.2, citing LIMA)
The paper endorses the LIMA finding (Zhou et al., 2023): 1,000–3,000 high-quality instruction pairs outperform 50,000 noisy pairs for fine-tuning. My Week 10 trace pool gives me ~12 high-quality traces; the probe library gives 34 seed scenarios. Rather than generating 10,000 weak variants, I authored 183 tasks with tight quality gates (judge filter: input coherence ≥4/5, verifiability ≥4/5, rubric clarity ≥4/5). This is consistent with the paper's Lesson 1: "a well-curated 500-task dataset beats a poorly filtered 50,000-task dataset for evaluation benchmarks."

### 2. Seed diversity matters (Section 3.4)
The paper argues that instruction diversity — not volume — drives generalization. I applied this by using four distinct authoring modes (trace-derived, programmatic parameter sweeps, LLM-synthetic, hand-authored adversarial) rather than generating all tasks from a single prompt template. The hand-authored adversarial slice (121 tasks) forces the evaluator to confront scenarios that templates cannot produce — specifically, tasks where the agent's error is in the relationship between a claim and a public record (P-031: CTO published a post contradicting the gap claim).

### 3. Evaluation data needs harder contamination controls (Section 5.1)
The paper dedicates a section to why evaluation datasets have stricter contamination requirements than training datasets. I apply three checks (N-gram, embedding, time-shift) per the methodology, with thresholds from the Chen et al. (2025) contamination survey. The paper's warning about "template leak" — when tasks share structural templates across partitions — is exactly what my contamination check found: 23/38 held-out tasks have N-gram violations because they share the Tenacious brand phrase "We staff specialized capability-gap squads."

## One Specific Disagreement

**Section 4.1 — "Multi-model routing should prioritise diversity of model families over capability."** The paper recommends routing to models from different organisations (e.g., GPT-class, Gemini-class, Claude-class) even if some are weaker, because family-diversity produces better distributional coverage. I partially disagree for the Tenacious use case. The failure modes I'm targeting — competitor gap fabrication, signal over-claiming — require the synthesis model to *understand the failure* in order to generate a compelling but wrong output. A weaker model that does not understand the Tenacious sales context produces outputs that are obviously wrong (generic spam), not adversarially wrong (grounded-sounding falsehood). For my hard adversarial slice, I use Claude Sonnet 4.6 as the synthesis model despite it also being the judge for spot-checks — which requires me to gate the adversarial tasks through a different judge (Qwen3-Next) to prevent leakage.

**Evidence:** On 10 pilot adversarial tasks generated by a cheap-tier model (Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B alone), 7/10 were obviously wrong (non-specific, clearly fabricated). On 10 generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6, 9/10 contained plausible-sounding but verifiably false claims — the kind that would defeat a surface-level evaluator. The paper's family-diversity recommendation is correct for bulk task generation but not for the hardest adversarial slice.

**Design implication:** For the hand-authored adversarial slice, I used a stronger synthesis model (Claude Sonnet 4.6) for seed generation, then rotated to Qwen3-Next for judging. The paper's diversity recommendation is applied to the programmatic and llm_synthetic slices.

## What I Would Add to the Paper

The paper's evaluation section (Section 5) focuses on decontamination techniques but does not discuss how to handle *inherent domain vocabulary leakage* — when the benchmark domain (e.g., Tenacious sales outreach) uses a small, distinctive vocabulary ("bench summary", "hiring signal brief") that will necessarily appear in both training and evaluation data. A recommendation for domain-specific contamination thresholds calibrated to vocabulary size would be a useful addition.
